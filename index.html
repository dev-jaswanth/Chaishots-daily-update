Executive Summary

February 2026 was a breakout month for Chai Shots. Total revenue reached ₹1.11 Crore — an 84% increase over January's ₹60.6L. This was driven by a compound effect: installs grew 43%, conversion rate improved by 2.8pp to 14.1%, and the renewal base expanded 69%. The month exhibited a clear pattern: a strong mid-month peak (W2 hitting ₹34.6L) followed by a gradual deceleration into W4 (₹25.2L) as install volumes tapered and a massive month-end expiry cohort (6,206 subs on Feb 28 alone) pressured renewal rates down.

The most significant structural shift: engagement rates jumped from 16.8% (Jan) to 24.6% (Feb) — a 46% improvement — signaling that the product is activating users more effectively. However, this engagement gain hasn't translated into renewal rate improvement. On a volume-weighted basis, renewal rate actually declined from 54.2% (Jan) to 52.6% (Feb) — a -1.6pp drop masked by the daily average (54.5%). The Feb 28 month-end expiry spike (6,206 subs) dragged aggregate renewal performance down significantly.

Week-over-Week Revenue Waterfall

The W2 Peak & W4 Decline: Feb W2 was the month's zenith — install volumes surged 55% WoW (likely a paid acquisition push) and conversion held strong at 14.9%. The subsequent decline through W3 and W4 tracks directly to install volume reduction (-28% W4 vs W2), not conversion quality. Conversion actually recovered 0.6pp in W4.
W4 Renewal Rate Collapse: The most concerning signal is W4's aggregate renewal rate dropping to 47.5% from W3's 56.5% (-9.0pp). Engaged RR crashed from 78.1% to 59.7%. This is explained by the Feb 28 month-end expiry spike — 6,206 subs expired on a single day (vs avg 1,500/day), overwhelming the renewal engine. The sheer volume diluted rates even though absolute renewal numbers hit their weekly peak (7,253).

Install Volatility: Daily installs ranged from 8,831 (Feb 28) to 20,201 (Feb 12) — a 2.3x range. The sharp drop from W2 peaks (18K+/day) to W4 lows (9-12K/day) suggests either budget pacing changes or campaign fatigue. The Feb 12 peak coincided with the month's highest revenue day (₹5.6L).

Engagement Breakthrough: Engagement jumped from 16.8% → 24.6% — a 46% improvement. This is the most significant structural change in February. More users are actively consuming content before their renewal window, which should translate to higher future renewal rates as this cohort matures.
Renewal Rate Paradox: Despite engagement improving 46%, overall renewal rate barely moved (+0.4pp). This is explained by the denominator effect — expiring subs grew 74.5% (22,697 → 39,602), diluting the rate. The absolute number of renewals actually grew 69.3%, nearly keeping pace with expiries.
Engaged RR Declining: Engaged renewal rate fell from 76.1% (Jan) to 73.5% (Feb), with a sharp W4 drop to 59.7%. The Feb 28 spike (6,206 expiries) is the primary culprit — engaged RR on that day was 43.0%, dragging the weekly average. When excluding Feb 28, W4 engaged RR is ~62%, still below W1's 82.1% but less alarming.
ARPU Compression on Renewals: Revenue per engaged renewal dropped from ₹121.53 (Jan) to ₹118.41 (Feb), and not-engaged renewal ARPU fell more steeply from ₹117.09 to ₹106.02. This suggests either plan-mix shifts toward lower-priced plans on renewal, or promotional pricing affecting renewal revenue.
Why Did Each Thing Happen?
Causal analysis connecting every major February movement to its root driver

1. Why did revenue nearly double (+84%)?

Revenue is a compound function: Installs × Conversion × ARPU for new subs, plus Expiring × Renewal Rate × ARPU for renewals. All three acquisition inputs improved simultaneously:

The compound effect means each driver amplifies the others. A 43% install increase at a 25% higher conversion rate doesn't produce 43% more revenue — it produces 84% more.

2. Why did W2 (Feb 8-14) peak and then decline?

W2 averaged 18,165 installs/day — the month's highest. This correlates with a likely paid acquisition burst (Valentine's Week marketing is a common subscription app strategy). When the campaign spend normalized post-Valentine's, install volumes dropped 9.5% in W3 and another 28% in W4. Revenue mechanically follows install volume because new subs account for ~80% of revenue.

3. Why did conversion rate improve from 11.0% to 14.1%?

Three concurrent factors: (a) better traffic quality from performance marketing optimization — the fact that ARPU held steady proves quality didn't degrade; (b) potential product-side improvements in onboarding/paywall that increased trial-to-paid conversion; (c) seasonal effect — post-New Year resolution traffic typically converts better than holiday casual browsers.

4. Why did engagement jump from 16.8% to 24.6%?

This is the most structurally positive signal. Possible drivers: (a) content library improvements making the product stickier; (b) push notification or re-engagement campaign effectiveness; (c) the higher-converting user cohort from improved acquisition is inherently more engaged. The timing correlates — as conversion improved, the quality of users entering the subscriber base improved, which shows up as higher engagement weeks later.

5. Why did W4 aggregate renewal rate crash to 47.5%?

This is primarily a denominator problem, not a numerator problem. Feb 28 had 6,206 expiring subs (vs daily avg of ~1,500) — this is the cohort that subscribed ~30 days earlier when the app was seeing massive install spikes in late January. This cohort is: (a) larger than any prior expiry cohort, (b) exposed to the product for fewer average days (month-end vs mid-month subscribers), and (c) more likely to include trial/promotional users. The renewal rate on Feb 28 specifically was 44.0%, but the days before it (Feb 22-27) averaged ~50.4%, which is only slightly below W3's 56.5%. Note: daily average RR (49.2%) masks the true volume-weighted impact — the aggregate 47.5% more accurately reflects actual revenue pressure.

6. Why did renewal ARPU compress?

Engaged renewal ARPU dropped from ₹122 (Jan) to ₹118 (Feb), and not-engaged renewal ARPU fell more sharply from ₹117 to ₹106. Two likely causes: (a) plan-mix shift — as the subscriber base scales, a larger proportion may be on the ₹99/month plan vs quarterly/annual plans; (b) the rapidly growing base means more first-time renewals (at base plan prices) vs long-tenured users who may have been on higher-priced legacy plans.

7. Why is new subs revenue share increasing (76.1% → 79.6%)?

The acquisition engine is growing faster than the retention engine can absorb. New subs revenue grew 92.4% while renewals revenue grew 57.1%. This is expected in a hypergrowth phase — the renewal base lags acquisition by one billing cycle. As long as renewal rates hold, this ratio should normalize toward 60-70% new / 30-40% renewals over the next 2-3 months as the Feb subscriber cohort hits its first renewal window in March.
